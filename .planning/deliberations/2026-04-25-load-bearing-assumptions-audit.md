# Load-Bearing Assumptions Audit (as Discipline)

## What prompted this deliberation

After committing to a position (document-discipline as the binding Finding 1 reading) and proposing a slate of six operationalization actions, I gave the slate confidently. The user pushed:

> "Can you more thoroughly try to justify your operationalization recommendations, what are the load-bearing assumptions?"

The prompt named what was missing: I had named positions but not the assumptions underneath them. A confident slate of six recommendations was hiding fragility — some recommendations rested on assumptions that wouldn't survive scrutiny.

## What the deliberation surfaced

### The audit pattern

For each recommendation in the slate, surface:

1. **Load-bearing assumptions** — the propositions the recommendation depends on for its warrant.
2. **What changes if the assumption is wrong** — the strongest version of the counter-position.
3. **Confidence calibration** — how much the assumption is supported by evidence vs. by my own pattern-matching.
4. **Honest revision** — whether the recommendation survives, qualifies, or retracts under the audit.

The discipline can be applied per recommendation or to the slate as a whole.

### The worked example

The audit on the six-recommendation slate produced:

| # | Recommendation | After audit |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `007/HANDOFF.md` retroactive override annotation | Survives — strongest evidential warrant |
| 2 | Reconcile suite contract / methodology now | **Walked back** — premature; the conflict may be layered procedure rather than real conflict; let `008` inform |
| 3 | `008/DESIGN.md` preamble specifying configurations | **Softened** — flag the asymmetric surface; let the spike author decide where to specify and when |
| 4 | Drop the larger pressure-pass remedies | Survives — most likely-to-look-wrong-in-retrospect, but the textual evidence supports |
| 5 | Self-review Findings 5 and 7 | **Retracted** — self-review of contestable framings doesn't work; the paired review just demonstrated this |
| 6 | Calibrated register going forward | Survives as a writing-discipline note (not a remedy) |

The audit walked the slate from six confident recommendations to three concrete actions + two retractions/walkbacks + one writing note. The walk-back was *the audit doing its job*, not a failure.

### The cross-cutting assumptions

Below the per-recommendation assumptions sit cross-cutting ones:

- **Trace integrity matters more than experimental velocity.** This privileges document-discipline over running `008`.
- **The judgment that document-discipline is Finding 1's primary reading is mine.** The user could reasonably push back: that's a methodology call, not a writing call.
- **Both reviewers' textual citations are correct.** Almost certainly true (they quoted specific lines), but not infallible.

The deepest assumption: **the right response to a paired review that surfaced overreach is more careful local work, not less work.** The alternative ("drop everything, run `008`, see what happens") is also defensible.

### Why this is reusable

The pattern is recipe-shaped:

1. List the recommendations / proposed actions / claims.
2. Per item: name 2-4 assumptions; describe what changes if each is wrong; calibrate confidence.
3. List cross-cutting assumptions that span items.
4. Identify any deepest assumptions that the whole slate rests on.
5. Walk back, soften, or retract any items where the audit reveals the assumptions are weaker than the prescriptive language implied.

It's reusable wherever someone — myself or another agent — has produced a confident slate that hasn't been audited.

## What was decided

**Discipline:** Any time a slate of more than ~3 actions, recommendations, findings, or remedies is proposed, run the load-bearing-assumptions audit before treating the slate as committed. The audit is cheap (~15-30 minutes per slate) and reliably surfaces over-confident framing.

**Trigger conditions** for running the audit:

- A slate of recommendations is being proposed for commitment.
- The slate's prescriptive language ("Forces:", "must", "critical") is doing rhetorical work.
- The slate emerged quickly, without explicit assumption-naming.
- The author is in audit mode and the slate is the audit's output.

## Status of follow-through

**Followed through in this session.** The audit walked the slate down from 6 to a smaller, better-grounded set. The discipline is now articulated and available for reuse.

## Open questions

- **When should the audit fire automatically vs. on prompt?** Probably automatic when proposing slates of remedies in audit/critique work. Optional otherwise. The user shouldn't have to ask "what are the load-bearing assumptions" — that's an admission the audit was skipped.
- **How does the audit interact with mediation-as-default?** The audit can become its own mediation-as-default if it's used to *avoid* taking positions ("here are five things, each of which has assumptions that may be wrong, so I won't commit"). The audit's outputs should *inform* position-taking, not replace it.

## Workflow-level lesson

**Slates of recommendations need their assumptions surfaced before commitment.** Confident prescriptive language is a signal that the audit hasn't happened yet. Doing the audit costs little and reliably catches over-confident framings.
